What Backtesting Can and Cannot Tell You

May 10, 2026

A backtest is a historical simulation

Backtesting asks a simple question: what would have happened if this strategy had been used in the past? It is a useful question, but it is not the same as asking what will happen in the future.

A good backtest can reveal whether an idea has any historical structure. It can show drawdowns, volatility, sensitivity, and failure periods. It can help compare variations before risking real money.

What it can tell you

A backtest can tell you how a rule behaved under known conditions. It can show whether returns came from many small wins or a few lucky trades. It can show whether the strategy survived different market regimes or only worked during one convenient window.

What it cannot tell you

A backtest cannot prove that a strategy will work live. Markets adapt. Liquidity changes. Data quality differs. Execution is imperfect. The future does not owe the strategy the same patterns that existed in the past.

It also cannot protect you from overfitting. If you test enough variations, one of them will look good by chance.

The practical use

Backtesting is best used as a filter, not a guarantee. It can eliminate weak ideas and sharpen promising ones. The next step is always live testing at small size, with realistic costs and humility about uncertainty.
